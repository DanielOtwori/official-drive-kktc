Why we built this

The good roads here were never the highways.

Northern Cyprus is a small place with an unreasonable amount packed into it — a mountain range with a castle on nearly every summit, a peninsula that runs eighty kilometres into open water, and villages where the recipe for a dessert hasn't changed since before the harbour was built. None of it is arranged for convenience. It rewards a car, a full tank, and no fixed schedule.

Every route on this site was driven, not desk-researched: timed at the wheel, photographed at the hour we recommend you go, and checked against what the people who live along it actually eat, watch, and avoid telling tour guides.

Route 01 — Photography · Scenic Views · Nature

Sunrise Viewpoints of Northern Cyprus

Six ridgelines timed for the twenty minutes when the Kyrenia range turns the colour of the inside of a shell.

Km driven
Full day
Difficulty
Best season
Stops

You leave Kyrenia while the harbour cats are still the only ones awake, headlights catching nothing but eucalyptus bark and the occasional startled partridge. The road climbs almost immediately, the switchbacks tightening the way they do on every good mountain road, and somewhere around the fourth hairpin the Mediterranean drops away behind you like a curtain being pulled back on a much bigger stage. This is the drive that convinces people the Kyrenia range deserves to be mentioned in the same breath as anywhere in the Aegean.

St. Hilarion appears first, its walls still holding the blue-grey of pre-dawn, and if you time it right you'll have the lower bailey to yourself for a full twenty minutes before the first coach groans up the access road. From there the route doesn't so much connect viewpoints as string them along a single spine of limestone, each one angled slightly differently at the sea, so that the same sunrise looks like four different paintings depending on which ruin you're standing in.

What makes this a photographer's route rather than a sightseeing one is the discipline it demands: you are chasing a twenty-minute window of light at each stop, which means you'll skip the gift shops, skip the guided groups, and instead learn to read a mountain by its shadows — where the light pools first, where it takes until nine to reach the valley floor. By the time you're winding back down through pine forest toward Kantara in the early afternoon, the same landscape that looked ink-blue at 5 a.m. has turned the flat, bleached gold of a Cyprus midday, and you'll understand why every stop on this route insists on a specific hour.

Bring layers. The ridge is ten degrees colder than the coast before eight, and the wind at Buffavento has ended more than one photographer's patience before their tripod was even level.

01

St. Hilarion Castle

Arrive 05:30Allow 90 min

Three tiers of Byzantine and Crusader fortification climb nearly 700 metres above the coast, and the walk up through the lower ward before the ticket office opens is worth doing slowly. The castle's silhouette, said to have influenced Disney's animators for Sleeping Beauty's towers, is genuinely stranger and more asymmetric than that story suggests — a working fortress rebuilt over four centuries by Byzantines, Lusignans and Venetians, not a fairytale set. From the upper enclosure the whole northern coastline unrolls at once, Kyrenia harbour a comma of masts far below. Arrive before the gates open and you'll have the western battlements to yourself as the sky moves from ink to rose. This is the only stop on the route where the light show happens twice — once over the sea at dawn, once again over the ruins themselves as the sun clears the ridge an hour later.

Best light: 05:45–06:30, west battlements
02

Bellapais

Arrive 07:15Allow 45 min

The village exists almost entirely because of its abbey, and the short detour here is less about the ruins — you'll see those properly on Route 05 — than about the terrace views from the square, where the ground drops away toward the sea so sharply that the whole village feels balanced on a shelf. Lawrence Durrell lived here in the 1950s and wrote about the plane trees in the square with an obsessiveness you'll understand once you're standing under them with a coffee. Come this early and the only sound is the abbey's stray cats arguing over territory. The stone here catches morning light in a way that turns almost amber by 7:30, which is your actual reason for stopping.

Best light: golden stone glow, 07:15–07:45
03

Buffavento Castle

Arrive 08:30Allow 2 hrs

The name means "buffeted by winds," and the climb — a steep, uneven 45-minute walk from the car park with no shade — earns every syllable of it. At 954 metres this is the highest of the island's three mountain castles, built to relay signals to St. Hilarion and Kantara using fire and mirrors long before anyone had a word for telecommunications. Almost nothing of the interior survives, which is exactly the point: what's left is a scramble of walls fused into the rock itself, and a 360-degree view that takes in both the northern and, on a clear day, the southern coast simultaneously. Few visitors make the climb, which means the summit is one of the quietest places on this entire route.

Best light: clear-air visibility peaks mid-morning
04

Kyrenia Harbour

Arrive 11:00Allow 1 hr

After three mountain stops, the horseshoe harbour feels almost domestic — Venetian and Ottoman facades in ochre and cream wrapped around a marina of wooden gulets. This isn't the sunrise moment; it's the counterpoint, the reminder that the range you've been climbing since dawn drops straight into the sea a few kilometres away. The 16th-century castle guarding the harbour mouth houses the wreck of a 4th-century BC merchant ship, one of the oldest ever recovered intact, and the quay itself is where locals actually take their own visiting family — not the restaurant strip facing the water, but the quieter benches near the old customs house.

Best light: midday reflections on calm water
05

Kantara Castle

Arrive 13:30Allow 90 min

The easternmost and lowest of the three mountain castles, Kantara guards the narrow neck of land where the Kyrenia range gives way to the Karpaz peninsula, and its position means you can see two coastlines from a single wall walk — something none of the other viewpoints on this route manage. It surrendered to Richard the Lionheart's forces without much of a fight in 1191, and later served as a lookout for Genoese and Ottoman raiders. The approach road is the best-maintained of the three castle climbs, which makes this a reasonable stop even for travellers who found Buffavento's scramble too much. Afternoon haze softens the light here rather than sharpening it — a different, gentler kind of photograph than the dawn stops.

Best light: hazy gold, 13:30–14:30
06

Esentepe Coastline

Arrive 16:30Allow flexible

The route ends where the mountains finally relent and meet the sea in a long, undeveloped stretch of coast backed by citrus groves and, from May to September, one of the Mediterranean's most important loggerhead turtle nesting beaches. There's no castle here, no ticket booth — just a wide gravel pull-off where fishermen park their trucks and the coast road curves out toward the Karpaz. It's a deliberately quiet way to end a route that started at 954 metres: sea level, engine off, the day's climbing behind you. Come back after dark in nesting season and, with a licensed guide only, you may see hatchlings make their run to the water.

Best light: long shadows, golden hour

Route 02 — Culture · Villages · Local Life

Hidden Villages of the Kyrenia Mountains

A slow loop through six settlements the coast road was built specifically to bypass.

Km driven
Full day
Difficulty
Best season
Stops

There's a version of this drive that takes ninety minutes if you keep to the main road, and there's the version this route describes, which takes most of a day because it keeps stopping the car for no reason a satnav would understand. The Kyrenia mountains hide a string of villages built into the northern slopes, close enough to the coast to smell the sea and far enough up that the coast road's traffic never reaches them. Most visitors never turn off toward a single one.

What connects these six places isn't a theme so much as a pace: stone houses with carved lintels, walnut and carob trees old enough to have outlived several empires, a working watermill or two, and coffeehouses where the same six men have been playing tavla at the same table for what looks like decades. The road between them narrows to a single lane more than once, climbs through pine, drops into citrus groves, and rarely straightens out for more than a few hundred metres — this is a route that punishes anyone in a hurry and rewards everyone who isn't.

You'll eat better on this route than on any of the others, almost by accident, because there's no tourist strip to avoid — just whichever kitchen happens to have its door open when you arrive hungry. Bring cash in small notes; several of the best stops don't have a card machine and have never seen the need for one.

01

Bellapais Village

MorningAllow 1 hr

Beyond the abbey that draws day-trippers, the village itself is a maze of stepped lanes, whitewashed courtyards and bougainvillea trained over stone doorways that's easy to miss if you drive straight through toward the ruins. This is where the route earns its name — walk two streets back from the main square and you're in a residential village that happens to have one of the finest Gothic ruins in the eastern Mediterranean as its parish landmark. Look for the carved wooden doors on the older houses; several date to the Ottoman period and are still in daily use. Locals gather at the square's cafés from mid-morning, and it's a good barometer for how the rest of the day's villages will feel.

Photo note: stepped lanes east of the square
02

Ozanköy

Late morningAllow 45 min

Once known as Kazafani, Ozanköy sits just above Kyrenia but feels a full mountain range removed from it — a village of orange and lemon groves irrigated by channels that still follow their Ottoman-era routes. The old village core has some of the best-preserved traditional Cypriot houses on the north coast, with deep eaves built for shade rather than style. There's a small Sunday market worth timing your visit around if your dates allow it, selling produce that never makes it as far as the coastal towns. The village war memorial and adjoining square give a good, unhurried view back down toward Kyrenia's harbour through the citrus canopy.

Photo note: citrus groves, best in fruiting season Nov–Feb
03

Karaman

MiddayAllow 1 hr

Karaman, historically known as Karmi, is the most deliberately preserved village on the route — many of its stone houses were restored under a conservation scheme decades ago and are now occupied by a mixed community of Cypriot and international residents who maintain the original architecture strictly. The result is a village that looks almost too composed to be real: narrow cobbled lanes, carved stone arches, geraniums in old olive-oil tins on every windowsill. A small on-site museum near the village centre gives context on Bronze Age tombs discovered in the surrounding hillside, which predate every other stop on this route by several thousand years.

Photo note: cobbled lane near the restored square, midday shade
04

Esentepe

Early afternoonAllow 1 hr

Formerly Agios Amvrosios, Esentepe spreads across the mountainside above the coastline you'll see properly on Route 01, and it's the largest and most workaday of the six villages — a proper high street with a butcher, a bakery and a mosque converted from a Byzantine-era church, its bell tower still visible under the minaret's addition. This is less a postcard stop than a chance to watch an ordinary Cypriot mountain town go about its afternoon, which is exactly why it belongs on the route. The village's carob-processing history is still visible in a couple of old storage buildings near the lower road.

Photo note: converted church-mosque bell tower
05

Çatalköy

Mid-afternoonAllow 45 min

Çatalköy, once Ayios Epiktitos, splits into an upper mountain village and a lower coastal extension, and it's the upper section — cooler, quieter, wrapped in pine — that belongs on this drive. A handful of restored watermills along the stream that runs through the village once processed grain for the wider district; two are still walkable and give a clear sense of how mountain villages here were engineered around water long before electricity arrived. The upper square has one of the better unbranded viewpoints on the whole route, looking straight down the wooded valley to the sea.

Photo note: old watermills along the stream path
06

Karaağaç

Late afternoonAllow flexible

The smallest and least visited village on the route, Karaağaç takes its name — "black tree" — from an ancient elm still standing near its centre, said to be several hundred years old and the unofficial meeting point for the entire settlement. There's no square to speak of, just a scatter of stone houses along a single lane and a coffeehouse that opens when its owner feels like it. This is the deliberate anticlimax the route is built toward: no monument, no view, just the quietest possible end to a day spent in villages that rarely see a rental car at all.

Photo note: the old elm, late golden light

Route 03 — Road Trip · Beaches · Nature

Karpaz Scenic Coastal Drive

Eighty kilometres of peninsula, wild donkeys, and a monastery standing at the very last edge of the island.

Km driven
Full day
Difficulty
Best season
Stops

The Karpaz peninsula doesn't announce itself so much as gradually take over. Somewhere past İskele the roadside development thins, then stops altogether, and for the next hour of driving there's nothing on either side but scrub, carob trees, and the sea flashing between low dunes. This is the longest route in the collection for a reason: the Karpaz rewards distance. It's the one part of the island that still looks, in long stretches, exactly as it must have a century ago.

The road itself does the storytelling. It narrows twice, loses its centre line entirely for a few kilometres near the peninsula's tip, and shares space with the region's famous feral donkeys, descendants of animals abandoned by farming families decades ago and now a semi-protected fixture of the landscape — expect at least one roadside encounter and budget the ten minutes it'll cost you to take photographs. Villages here are smaller and further apart than anywhere else on the island, which is precisely what preserves the sense that you're driving toward somewhere genuinely remote rather than another resort.

Golden Beach, roughly two-thirds of the way along, is the payoff most people come for: kilometres of empty sand backed by dunes, still one of the least developed beaches in the Eastern Mediterranean and an internationally important nesting site for green and loggerhead turtles. But the route's actual ending, at the monastery of Apostolos Andreas on the peninsula's final headland, is the quieter and more affecting of the two — the literal end of the road, with nothing beyond it but open water toward the Turkish coast. Plan to leave before dusk; there's no lighting on the return stretch and the donkeys don't respect headlights.

01

İskele

08:00 departureAllow 30 min

The gateway town to the peninsula, İskele — historically Trikomo — is where the route properly begins, both as your last chance at a full-service fuel stop and as an easy introduction to the region's Byzantine layer: the Church of Panagia Theotokos, with fragments of its original frescoes, sits quietly just off the main road. It's a working town rather than a tourist stop, and that's the correct register to arrive in — the Karpaz ahead of you is not going to be curated for visitors, and İskele is the last place on the route that is.

Photo note: church exterior, morning side light
02

Büyükkonuk

09:00Allow 1 hr

Known locally as Komi Kebir, Büyükkonuk was one of the first villages on the island to formally develop agrotourism, and it shows in the best way — restored stone farmhouses, small producers selling carob syrup and thyme honey directly from courtyards, and a genuine working-village atmosphere rather than a staged one. The annual Agrotourism Festival each September draws visitors from across the island for exactly this reason. Even outside festival season, it's worth the short detour off the main peninsula road to see how a Karpaz farming village actually functions.

Photo note: stone farmhouse courtyards, mid-morning
03

Kantara Castle

10:30Allow 1 hr

Revisited from Route 01 but experienced entirely differently here, Kantara marks the true beginning of the Karpaz — the point where the Kyrenia range finally tapers into the flat spine of the peninsula ahead. From the walls you can trace, in a single sweep, almost the entire drive still in front of you: the narrowing land, the coast on both sides simultaneously, the peninsula fading blue-grey into distance. If you did Route 01, treat this as a checkpoint rather than a repeat; the light and the view's meaning are both completely different at mid-morning, looking east instead of west.

Photo note: east-facing walls, peninsula overview
04

Dipkarpaz

12:30Allow 1 hr

The last proper town before the peninsula empties out completely, Dipkarpaz (Rizokarpaso) is unusual on the island for its mixed community, including one of the few remaining Greek Cypriot populations in the north, centred around the Church of Ayios Synesios. It's a good, unhurried lunch stop — the town's small restaurants serve fish landed that morning rather than trucked in — and a last chance to fuel up and buy water before the empty final third of the route. The town's slower pace is a preview of what's ahead: this is where the Karpaz stops being a destination and starts being a landscape.

Photo note: Ayios Synesios church, early afternoon
05

Golden Beach

14:00Allow 2 hrs

Nangomi Bay, universally known as Golden Beach, is roughly six kilometres of soft sand backed by dunes and scrub, with no permanent buildings on most of its length — a genuine rarity on a Mediterranean coastline. It's one of the most important nesting beaches in the region for green and loggerhead turtles, monitored seasonally by conservation volunteers whose small research hut is usually the only structure in sight. Swim, but be aware there are no lifeguards and currents can be stronger than the calm surface suggests. This is the stop to simply stop moving for two hours; nothing else on the route asks you to.

Photo note: dune-backed sand, late afternoon light
06

Apostolos Andreas Monastery

16:30Allow 1 hr

The road ends, quite literally, at this monastery on the peninsula's final headland — one of the most significant pilgrimage sites in Cypriot Orthodox tradition, associated with Saint Andrew and a freshwater spring said to have appeared miraculously on this otherwise dry, rocky point. The current buildings date mainly to the 19th century, restored in stages, but the site's religious significance goes back centuries further. Standing at the sea wall below the monastery, with open water on three sides and nothing but a lighthouse further out, is as close as this route gets to a full stop — geographically and otherwise.

Photo note: sea wall at dusk, before the light fails

Route 04 — Food · Culture · Local Experiences

Traditional Cypriot Dessert Trail

An afternoon built entirely around sugar, carob and orange blossom water, and the bakers who never wrote a recipe down.

Km driven
Half day
Difficulty
Best season
Stops

This route breaks the usual rule about starting early. You want the ovens already working, the markets already full, and — for the final two stops — the light already turning toward evening, which is when Cypriot dessert culture actually happens: after the day's heat, not before it. It begins inland, in Lefkoşa's walled old city, and drifts steadily toward the coast, following roughly the route sugar and citrus took historically as they moved from inland groves to the harbour towns that shipped them.

What you're tasting along the way isn't a single cuisine so much as several centuries layered on top of each other — Ottoman-era şerbetli desserts soaked in syrup, Venetian-influenced almond pastes, British colonial-era teahouse habits that never fully left, and underneath all of it, the carob and citrus that grow wild across the whole island and turn up in nearly everything sweet made here. Locals will tell you, often with some heat, exactly which bakery does the definitive version of a given dessert, and the disagreements are part of the culture.

Pace yourself. This is a route best done with an empty stomach and a willingness to split portions — six stops of full-sized desserts will defeat anyone. Bring cash for the market stalls, and don't be surprised if a shopkeeper insists you try something you didn't order; refusing is considered stranger than accepting.

01

Lefkoşa Old City

11:00Allow 1.5 hrs

Inside the Venetian walls, the old city's narrow lanes hide several generations-old sweet shops specialising in şeker macunu — sugared fruit and vegetable preserves, made from everything from bergamot to baby aubergine, a tradition that predates modern confectionery entirely and is rarely found outside Cyprus. This is also the best place on the island for künefe, a shredded pastry soaked in syrup and layered with unsalted cheese, served hot enough that the cheese still stretches. The Büyük Han, a restored 16th-century caravanserai a short walk away, is worth combining with this stop for context on the trade routes that first brought sugar and spice to the island.

Try: künefe served straight from the tray, hot
02

Kyrenia Harbour Cafés

13:00Allow 1 hr

Skip the harbourfront terraces with the laminated menus and head one street back, where a handful of older cafés serve mahalepi — a chilled, milky pudding often flavoured with rosewater and topped with crushed pistachio — alongside proper Cyprus coffee, brewed unfiltered in a small copper pot (cezve) and served with the grounds still settling at the bottom. Locals order it by sweetness level, not size, and it's worth learning the terms (sade, orta, şekerli) before you order. The contrast between the harbour's polished waterfront and these plainer back-street cafés is itself part of what this stop is trying to show you.

Try: mahalepi with Cyprus coffee, sade or orta
03

Bellapais Village Bakeries

14:30Allow 45 min

A short climb from Kyrenia, Bellapais has two family bakeries, a few doors apart, both making a version of a walnut-and-carob-molasses pastry that appears nowhere else on the route and that neither will confirm they got the recipe from the other. It's dense, not overly sweet, and pairs better with unsweetened tea than with coffee — a useful palate reset after two stops of syrup-heavy desserts. Buy a small amount to eat on the terrace with a view down toward the sea; it doesn't travel especially well and is best eaten within the hour.

Try: walnut and carob-molasses pastry, eaten warm
04

Güzelyurt Local Markets

16:00Allow 1 hr

Güzelyurt sits in the heart of the island's citrus belt, and its produce market is where the raw ingredients behind half the desserts on this trail actually come from — oranges, mandarins and the region's distinctive bitter orange, used to make marmalades and candied peel found nowhere else in the same concentration. A small museum near the town centre documents the citrus industry's history, but the market itself, especially in the winter harvest months, is the real attraction: crates of fruit stacked shoulder-height, and vendors happy to let you taste before you buy.

Try: candied bitter-orange peel, straight from the stall
05

Famagusta Traditional Sweet Shops

17:30Allow 1 hr

Within the walls of Famagusta's old town, a cluster of long-running sweet shops near the covered bazaar make lokum (Turkish delight) by hand in flavours particular to this side of the island — bergamot, mastic, and a rosewater-and-almond combination rarely exported. Several shops let you watch the setting and cutting process through the counter glass, which is worth timing your visit around if the shopkeeper is working when you arrive. Buy more than you think you need; it's one of the few desserts on this route that genuinely travels well.

Try: bergamot or mastic lokum, watch it cut fresh
06

İskele Village Cafés

19:00Allow flexible

The trail ends in İskele as evening settles in, at a small cluster of cafés that serve şam tatlısı — a syrup-soaked semolina cake distantly related to versions found across the former Ottoman world, but made here with local orange blossom water that gives it a noticeably different finish. This is the route's deliberate wind-down stop: outdoor tables, slower service, and the kind of unhurried evening café culture that doesn't really exist on the tourist-facing coast. It's also the natural jumping-off point if you're continuing straight into Route 03 the following morning.

Try: şam tatlısı with unsweetened tea, after dark

Route 05 — History · Architecture · Heritage

Historic Monasteries & Churches Route

Nine centuries of ruin, from a Gothic abbey in the mountains to a basilica standing open to the sky at Salamis.

Km driven
Full day
Difficulty
Best season
Stops

Northern Cyprus keeps its religious history remarkably exposed — not curated behind glass, but standing in fields, sitting open-roofed to the weather, still catching the same light it caught nine hundred years ago. This route strings together the island's major Christian sites, from a Gothic monastery in the mountains to a Roman-era city where a basilica's columns still stand in rows despite two millennia of earthquakes, invasions and neglect. It's the longest and most demanding route in the collection, and deliberately so: this history doesn't compress into an afternoon.

What's striking, driving between these sites in a single day, is how many distinct eras stack on top of each other in such a small area — a 13th-century Lusignan abbey, a monastery associated with one of Christianity's earliest missionary journeys, a Byzantine church still functioning as a small museum, and beneath much of it, Roman and even earlier foundations that the later builders simply built on top of rather than clearing away. Cyprus's position at the eastern edge of the Mediterranean meant almost every regional power that ever mattered passed through, and each one left a church, chapel or basilica behind.

Come in the cooler months if you can. Several of these sites, especially Salamis, offer almost no shade, and the ruins are far more atmospheric under grey winter light than the flattening glare of a Cyprus summer noon. Wear proper shoes — uneven Roman paving and loose Gothic masonry punish sandals more than any other route here.

01

Bellapais Abbey

08:30Allow 1.5 hrs

Founded by Augustinian canons in the 13th century under Lusignan rule, Bellapais is widely considered the finest example of Gothic architecture in the Eastern Mediterranean — its cloister and refectory survive largely intact, roofless in places but structurally coherent enough to picture exactly how the monastic community once used the space. The abbey earned its name, "Abbaye de la Paix," from a peace treaty said to have been settled here. Arrive at opening to see the cloister before tour groups fill it; the carved capitals along the arcade reward close attention, no two are identical. This is the earliest-built site on the route and, in many visitors' view, the most beautiful.

Photo note: cloister arcade, morning side light
02

St. Barnabas Monastery

10:30Allow 1.5 hrs

Built near the traditional tomb site of Saint Barnabas — co-founder of the Cypriot Orthodox Church and companion of Paul on his early missionary journeys — the current monastery buildings date mainly to the 18th century, though the site's association with the saint goes back to the 5th century. The domed church now functions partly as an icon museum, housing an extraordinary collection saved from churches across the region, displayed in the same building where they were once venerated. A short walk leads to the reputed tomb itself, a simple rock-cut chamber that contrasts sharply with the ornate church above it.

Photo note: domed courtyard, icon museum interior
03

Salamis

12:30Allow 2 hrs

Once the most important city on the island, Salamis was a major Roman and early Byzantine centre before earthquakes and Arab raids led to its slow abandonment from the 7th century onward. Its ruined basilica, dedicated to the city's patron and rebuilt multiple times after successive earthquakes, still stands with rows of columns visible against open sky — one of the most striking Roman-era ruins in the Eastern Mediterranean and largely uncrowded even in peak season. The adjoining gymnasium and theatre complex extends the visit considerably if time allows; budget extra if Roman archaeology particularly interests you.

Photo note: basilica columns against open sky, midday
04

Apostolos Andreas Monastery

15:30Allow 1 hr

Revisited here from Route 03 but framed differently on this route as a pilgrimage destination rather than a scenic endpoint — this is one of the most venerated Orthodox Christian sites in Cyprus, associated with a miraculous spring and Saint Andrew's own maritime journey. The current structures reflect 19th-century restoration over an older foundation, and the site remains an active place of pilgrimage and quiet reflection rather than a purely historical monument. Approaching it as the fourth stop on a long day of ruins gives it a different weight than arriving fresh — a working sacred site after several stops that are purely archaeological.

Photo note: interior courtyard, afternoon calm
05

St. Mamas Church

17:00Allow 45 min

In Güzelyurt, this Byzantine-era church, rebuilt in Gothic and later styles across several centuries, is dedicated to a hermit saint associated with an unusual legend involving a tax collector and a tamed lion, a story depicted in the church's icons. Now partly repurposed as an archaeology museum, it houses artefacts recovered from ancient Soli and other regional sites, giving the visit a dual purpose beyond the architecture itself. The building's layered construction — visible in the mismatched masonry of different eras — makes it a useful, condensed lesson in how Cypriot churches were rebuilt rather than replaced across centuries of changing rule.

Photo note: layered masonry detail, late afternoon
06

Kantara Castle

18:30Allow 1 hr

The route closes, appropriately, at the same castle that opens Route 03 — but here it functions as a final vantage point over nearly everything you've seen that day, and as the closest thing on the island to a defensive structure built explicitly to protect these religious sites from raiders. Arrive for sunset if the season allows; the castle's eastern walls catch the last light while the peninsula below fades into shadow, a fitting close to a day spent moving between the sacred and the ruined.

Photo note: eastern walls at sunset
